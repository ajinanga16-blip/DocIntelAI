# Google Developer Documentation Style Guide

## Purpose

To provide editorial guidelines for writing clear and consistent technical documentation for software developers and other technical practitioners.

## Audience

Software developers and technical practitioners.

## Tone

Professional and clear, prioritizing clarity and consistency.

## Voice

Direct and instructional, with flexibility to adapt for clarity and reader understanding.

## Writing Principles

- Prioritize clarity and consistency.
- Use project-specific guidelines first, then this guide, and finally third-party references.
- Adapt guidelines to suit the specific domain and audience needs.

## Headings

### Heading Rules

- Use headings to organize content logically.
- Ensure headings are descriptive and concise.

### Capitalization Rules

- Follow standard capitalization rules for headings, using title case.

## Terminology

### Preferred Terms

- Use terms that are widely accepted and understood within the technical community.

### Avoided Terms

- Avoid jargon and terms that are not widely recognized or could be confusing.

### Terminology Examples

- Use "sign in" instead of "log in."
- Prefer "select" over "click" when referring to UI interactions.

## Grammar

### Active / Passive Voice

- Prefer active voice for clarity and directness.

### Pronouns

- Use second person ("you") to directly address the reader.

### Sentence Length

- Keep sentences concise to enhance readability.

## Lists

### Bulleted Lists

- Use for items that do not require a specific order.

### Numbered Lists

- Use for sequential steps or ordered information.

## Procedures

### Task Writing

- Clearly define the task and its purpose.

### Step Writing

- Use numbered steps for clarity and follow a logical sequence.

## User Interface Language

### Buttons

- Use the exact label text from the UI.

### Menus

- Refer to menu items as they appear in the interface.

### Dialogs

- Clearly describe dialog options and actions.

### Messages

- Ensure messages are clear and actionable.

## Accessibility

### Inclusive Language

- Use language that is inclusive and respectful to all users.

### Bias-Free Language

- Avoid language that could be biased or discriminatory.

### Alt Text

- Provide descriptive alt text for images to support screen readers.

## Formatting

### Notes

- Use notes to provide additional information or tips.

### Warnings

- Clearly highlight warnings to alert users to potential issues.

### Code Examples

- Ensure code examples are clear, correct, and relevant.

### Links

- Use descriptive link text that indicates the link's destination.

## Examples

- Provide examples to illustrate complex concepts or procedures.

## Exceptions

- Deviate from guidelines when necessary for clarity or to better suit the audience's needs, ensuring consistency throughout the document.
