# Chicago Manual of Style

## Purpose
The Chicago Manual of Style provides comprehensive guidelines for writing, editing, and publishing, ensuring clarity, consistency, and accuracy in scholarly and professional works.

## Audience
The guide is intended for authors, editors, publishers, and scholars involved in the preparation and publication of books, journals, and other scholarly materials.

## Tone
The tone is formal and instructional, providing authoritative guidance on style and usage.

## Voice
The voice is authoritative and prescriptive, offering clear directives on best practices in writing and publishing.

## Writing Principles
- Emphasize clarity and accessibility in prose.
- Maintain accuracy and attention to detail.
- Adapt to technological and cultural shifts while upholding traditional standards.

## Headings

### Heading Rules
- Use running heads as signposts, especially in scholarly books and textbooks.
- Omit running heads on display pages, chapter openings, and pages with only illustrations or tables.

### Capitalization Rules
- Use lowercase roman numerals for front matter pagination.
- Use Arabic numerals for text and back matter pagination.

## Terminology

### Preferred Terms
- Use "email" without a hyphen.
- Use "internet" in lowercase.

### Avoided Terms
- Avoid outdated terms that do not reflect current usage or editorial preferences.

### Terminology Examples
- Use "US" as a noun, subject to editorial discretion.

## Grammar

### Active / Passive Voice
- The guide does not explicitly state a preference but emphasizes clarity and precision in sentence construction.

### Pronouns
- Recognize the use of "they" as a gender-neutral singular pronoun.

### Sentence Length
- Ensure sentences are clear and not overly complex to maintain readability.

## Lists

### Bulleted Lists
- Use for items that do not require a specific order.

### Numbered Lists
- Use for sequential steps or items that require a specific order.

## Procedures

### Task Writing
- Provide clear and concise instructions for tasks, ensuring they are easy to follow.

### Step Writing
- Use numbered steps for procedures that require a specific sequence.

### Inclusive Language
- Recognize and incorporate gender-neutral language where applicable.

### Bias-Free Language
- Avoid language that may perpetuate bias or stereotypes.

## Formatting

### Notes
- Use endnotes or footnotes for additional information, with running heads indicating page numbers or chapters.

## Exceptions

- Legal and public document citations are updated to reflect the latest standards, such as The Bluebook.
