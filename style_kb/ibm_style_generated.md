# IBM Style Guide

## Purpose
The IBM Style Guide provides conventions for writers and editors to ensure consistency, clarity, and quality in IBM's technical documentation.

## Audience
The guide is intended for writers, editors, information architects, managers, and consultants involved in creating, evaluating, or using IBM's technical documentation.

## Tone
The tone should be professional, clear, and concise, avoiding unnecessary complexity and jargon.

## Voice
The voice should be consistent across all documentation, reflecting IBM's authoritative and knowledgeable stance.

## Writing Principles
- Use abbreviations only when they are clear and make the information easier to understand.
- Avoid abbreviations that are unclear or infrequently used.
- Choose the indefinite article based on the pronunciation of the abbreviation.
- Do not use abbreviations as nouns or verbs.

## Headings

### Heading Rules
- Use clear and descriptive headings.
- Avoid abbreviations in headings unless they are widely recognized.

### Capitalization Rules
- Capitalize the first letter of each major word in headings.
- Follow specific capitalization rules for technical terms and interfaces.

## Terminology

### Preferred Terms
- Use terms that are widely recognized and understood by the target audience.

### Avoided Terms
- Avoid Latin abbreviations and abbreviations of IBM product names unless commonly recognized.

### Terminology Examples
- Use "HTML" instead of spelling it out if the audience is familiar with it.

## Grammar

### Active / Passive Voice
- Prefer active voice for clarity and directness.

### Pronouns
- Use gender-neutral pronouns and avoid ambiguous references.

### Sentence Length
- Keep sentences concise to enhance readability.

## Lists

### Bulleted Lists
- Use for unordered items.

### Numbered Lists
- Use for sequential steps or ordered items.

## Procedures

### Task Writing
- Introduce the procedure clearly and concisely.

### Step Writing
- Write steps in a clear, logical order, indicating optional and conditional steps as needed.

## User Interface Language

### Buttons
- Use the exact label text from the interface.

### Menus
- Refer to menu items as they appear in the interface.

### Dialogs
- Use dialog titles and button names as they appear.

### Messages
- Write messages that are clear and actionable.

## Accessibility

### Inclusive Language
- Use language that is inclusive and respectful of all users.

### Bias-Free Language
- Avoid language that could be considered biased or discriminatory.

### Alt Text
- Provide descriptive alt text for all images to ensure accessibility.

## Formatting

### Notes
- Use notes to provide additional information or tips.

### Warnings
- Clearly format warnings to alert users to potential issues.

### Code Examples
- Present code examples clearly and consistently.

### Links
- Use descriptive link text that indicates the link's destination.

## Examples
- Provide examples to clarify complex concepts or procedures.

## Exceptions
- Exceptions to these guidelines should be rare and justified by specific audience needs or technical requirements.
